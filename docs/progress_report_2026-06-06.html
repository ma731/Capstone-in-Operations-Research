Capstone in Operations Research · Weekly Progress

Does spatial correlation of carbon intensity help robust data-center scheduling?

A falsification test on a revised feasible set, replicated across two independent grids.

Student: Marco Ortiz Togashi Supervisor: Prof. Bissan Ghaddar Week ending: 6 June 2026 Method: Algorithm 2b — Mahalanobis–Wasserstein DRO (unchanged)
Headline result

Spatial correlation of carbon intensity adds no robust value to the schedule — a result that now replicates across two independent grids (California/Nevada and Iberia + France). Pairing the empirical joint covariance with the scheduler, versus a covariance with all cross-region structure destroyed, changes out-of-sample tail emissions (CVaR0.95) by at most 0.06 % in every regime and alpha level we tested. We show this null is substantive (the optimizer is not frozen — it retains 10–32 % reallocation room) rather than a mechanical artifact, and that it survives a shrinkage and residual-space robustness battery. Strikingly, in Iberia the most tightly-coupled pair (Spain–Portugal) has empirical correlation ≈ 0.85, higher than any California pair — and still yields no exploitable robust value.

1What we did this week

Two pieces of work, both designed so the conclusion would be trustworthy whichever way it came out.

Task A — a more defensible feasible set. The earlier model leaned on a single aggregate power cap that dominated the geometry and froze the operating point. We removed it and rebuilt the operational constraints from more physically grounded pieces: a job-deadline (deferral-deadline) constraint and a temperature-coupled cooling/PUE constraint, the latter driven by a new external dataset (hourly 2 m air temperature from the Open-Meteo ERA5 archive). We then re-ran the central experiment on this revised set, across three constraint regimes of increasing tightness, with full pre-registration discipline.

Task B — a second, independent grid. To test whether the finding is a quirk of California, we transported the exact machinery to Iberia + France (Spain, Portugal, France). Prof. Ghaddar had flagged that regions must be electrically coupled to be interesting; Spain and Portugal share the MIBEL market and are tightly coupled, which addresses that concern directly. The solver, the metric, the cross-validation, the bootstrap and the table format are all identical to Task A — only the region set, the local clock, and a single climate-dependent parameter differ — so the two cases sit side by side and are directly comparable.

The code is parameterized, tested (149 unit tests pass), and pushed to the repository. Every numeric claim below is reproduced from committed result files.

2The model & the DRO objective

We schedule compute across \(R\) regions over a \(T=24\)-hour day. The decision variable is a matrix \[ x \in \mathbb{R}^{R\times T}_{\ge 0}, \qquad x_{r,t} = \text{compute power placed in region } r \text{ at hour } t \;[\mathrm{MW}]. \] The carbon intensity of each region-hour, \(\rho_{r,t}\) (gCO\(_2\)eq/kWh), is the uncertain quantity. The carbon cost of a schedule is linear in \(\rho\): \[ \langle \rho, x\rangle \;=\; \sum_{r=1}^{R}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\rho_{r,t}\,x_{r,t}. \]

Distributional robustness

We do not trust a single forecast of \(\rho\). Instead we minimize the worst-case expected cost over an ambiguity set \(\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon(\hat{\mathbb{P}})\) — a Wasserstein ball of radius \(\varepsilon\) centered on the empirical distribution \(\hat{\mathbb{P}}\) of the daily carbon field: \[ \min_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\ \sup_{\mathbb{Q}\in\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon(\hat{\mathbb{P}})} \ \mathbb{E}_{\mathbb{Q}}\big[\langle \rho, x\rangle\big]. \] The radius \(\varepsilon\ge 0\) is how much we distrust the empirical distribution: \(\varepsilon=0\) trusts it completely, larger \(\varepsilon\) hedges against larger deviations.

For a cost that is linear in the uncertain \(\rho\), and a type-2 Wasserstein ball whose ground metric is the Mahalanobis distance induced by the empirical covariance \(\hat\Sigma\), the inner worst-case has a closed form (the standard Wasserstein-DRO duality result, Esfahani & Kuhn 2018): the worst case is the empirical mean cost plus a penalty equal to \(\varepsilon\) times the Mahalanobis norm of the schedule. The problem collapses to a finite-dimensional program:

\[ \boxed{\ \min_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\ \underbrace{\langle \bar\rho, x\rangle}_{\text{mean carbon}} \;+\; \underbrace{\varepsilon\,\sqrt{x^{\top}\hat\Sigma\,x}}_{\text{robustness penalty}}\ } \]

\(\bar\rho\in\mathbb{R}^{R\times T}\) is the empirical mean field (the average carbon intensity per region-hour over the training days). \(\hat\Sigma\in\mathbb{R}^{RT\times RT}\) is the empirical covariance of the flattened daily field \(\mathrm{vec}(\rho)\). The square-root term \(\sqrt{x^\top\hat\Sigma x}\) is the standard deviation of the schedule's carbon cost under \(\hat\Sigma\): it is large for schedules whose cost swings a lot when the carbon field deviates from its mean, and the penalty steers the optimizer away from them.

Why the covariance is where "spatial correlation" lives

Write \(\hat\Sigma\) in \(T\times T\) blocks indexed by region pairs: \[ \hat\Sigma=\begin{pmatrix}\Sigma_{11}&\Sigma_{12}&\cdots&\Sigma_{1R}\\ \Sigma_{21}&\Sigma_{22}&&\vdots\\ \vdots&&\ddots&\\ \Sigma_{R1}&\cdots&&\Sigma_{RR}\end{pmatrix},\qquad \Sigma_{rs}=\operatorname{Cov}\!\big(\rho_{r,\cdot},\,\rho_{s,\cdot}\big)\in\mathbb{R}^{T\times T}. \] The diagonal blocks \(\Sigma_{rr}\) are each region's own within-region temporal covariance. The off-diagonal blocks \(\Sigma_{rs}\,(r\ne s)\) are exactly the cross-region (spatial) covariance. Through the quadratic form \(x^\top\hat\Sigma x=\sum_{r,s} x_r^\top \Sigma_{rs}\, x_s\), those off-diagonal blocks are the only way spatial correlation can change the schedule. This is the structural fact the whole project hinges on, and the next section turns it into a clean experiment.

From quadratic to a second-order cone program (SOCP)

We never form the quadratic directly. Factor the (regularized) covariance by its Cholesky decomposition \(\hat\Sigma = L L^{\top}\) with \(L\) lower-triangular. Then \[ \sqrt{x^{\top}\hat\Sigma\,x}=\sqrt{x^{\top}LL^{\top}x}=\big\|L^{\top}\mathrm{vec}(x)\big\|_{2}, \] so the program becomes \[ \min_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\ \langle\bar\rho,x\rangle+\varepsilon\,\big\|L^{\top}\mathrm{vec}(x)\big\|_{2}, \] which, via its epigraph \(\min\ \langle\bar\rho,x\rangle+\varepsilon s\ \text{ s.t. }\ \|L^\top\mathrm{vec}(x)\|_2\le s\), is a second-order cone program — convex, and solved to global optimality by CLARABEL. The flattening \(\mathrm{vec}(x)_{rT+t}=x_{r,t}\) is fixed row-major everywhere so the block structure above is unambiguous. solve_mahalanobis_dro · algorithm_2b_mahalanobis.py

Orientation matters. The penalty must be \(\|L^{\top}\mathrm{vec}(x)\|_2\), not \(\|L\,\mathrm{vec}(x)\|_2\): the former equals \(x^\top(LL^\top)x=x^\top\hat\Sigma x\); the latter computes \(x^\top L^\top L\,x\), a different quadratic form. The code uses \(L^{\top}\) and pins it with a post-solve numerical cross-check, so a silent sign/orientation bug cannot pass.

3The revised feasible set \(\mathcal{X}\)

The robustness method above is held fixed. Only the feasible set \(\mathcal{X}\) was revised this week. Each constraint is linear, so the program stays an SOCP.

C0 — per-cell capacity ceiling (kept)

\[ 0\ \le\ x_{r,t}\ \le\ \bar x_{r,t}. \]

A site cannot draw more than its installed capacity in any hour. \(\bar x = 50\) MW per cell.

C2 — flexible / inflexible split (kept)

A fraction \(\alpha_r\in[0,1]\) of each region's daily work \(W_r\) is inflexible — it must run on a fixed intraday shape \(p_{r,t}\) (with \(\sum_t p_{r,t}=1\)) no matter what. The rest is flexible and is what the scheduler actually moves: \[ x_{r,t}=\underbrace{\alpha_r W_r\,p_{r,t}}_{\text{inflexible base }b_{r,t}}+\,x^{\mathrm{flex}}_{r,t},\qquad x^{\mathrm{flex}}_{r,t}\ge 0,\qquad \sum_{t} x^{\mathrm{flex}}_{r,t}=(1-\alpha_r)\,W_r. \]

\(\alpha\) is the master dial. Lower \(\alpha\) ⇒ more movable work ⇒ more room for any spatial effect to appear. We sweep \(\alpha\in\{0.30,\,0.50,\,0.75\}\). Work is set by a fixed utilization: \(W_r = 0.80\cdot\bar x\cdot T = 960\) MWh.

C3 — inter-hour ramp (kept)

\[ \big|\,x_{r,t}-x_{r,t-1}\,\big|\ \le\ \Delta_r,\qquad t=1,\dots,T-1. \]

Power cannot jump arbitrarily between adjacent hours (\(\Delta_r=15\) MW/h). This is what makes the naive "fill the cheapest hours first" schedule infeasible — it would slam from 0 to ceiling between hours.

C1 — aggregate power cap (DROPPED)

The old constraint \(\sum_r x_{r,t}\le P_{\max}\) is removed. It was a single global knob that flattened the polytope and was the main thing coupling regions in the deterministic problem; removing it makes the operating point depend on the more local constraints below.

3a — windowed-demand / deferral-deadline (NEW)

Deferrable jobs are not infinitely deferrable. For a deadline window \([\tau_1,\tau_2]\) and a fraction \(\gamma\), at least a \(\gamma\)-share of each region's flexible work must clear inside the window: \[ \sum_{t=\tau_1}^{\tau_2} x^{\mathrm{flex}}_{r,t}\ \ge\ \gamma\,(1-\alpha_r)\,W_r. \]

This is an aggregate deadline (a floor on how much flexible work has finished by the window's end), deliberately not a per-job service-level agreement — the correct, coarse, model-appropriate version of "don't defer everything to the single cheapest hour." We use the morning window \([\,0,7\,]\) (off the midday solar trough) with \(\gamma=0.20\).

3b — temperature-coupled thermal / PUE constraint (NEW)

Usable IT power is limited by effective (wall-plug) power, which is the IT power inflated by the facility's Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE). Cooling overhead rises with outdoor temperature through a piecewise-linear "hockey-stick": below an economizer set-point \(T_{\mathrm{set}}\) the site free-cools at a floor PUE; above it, mechanical cooling adds \(\kappa\) per degree: \[ \mathrm{PUE}(T)=\mathrm{PUE}_0+\kappa\,\max\!\big(T-T_{\mathrm{set}},\,0\big),\qquad \mathrm{PUE}\big(T^{\mathrm{air}}_{r,t}\big)\cdot x_{r,t}\ \le\ \bar P. \]

Because the air temperature \(T^{\mathrm{air}}_{r,t}\) is data (a fixed field, never re-estimated per fold and never read from the test set), \(\mathrm{PUE}(\cdot)\) is a known constant per cell and (3b) is linear — it simply tightens the ceiling to \(\bar P/\mathrm{PUE}(T^{\mathrm{air}}_{r,t})\) in hot hours, so the program stays an SOCP. Parameters sit in industry ranges: \(\mathrm{PUE}_0=1.10\), \(\kappa=0.015\,/^\circ\mathrm{C}\), \(\bar P=57\) MW; the economizer set-point is \(T_{\mathrm{set}}=20^\circ\mathrm{C}\) for California and the single re-calibrated value \(14^\circ\mathrm{C}\) for the milder Iberian climate (justified in §6).

4The falsification test: joint vs shuffled covariance

How do we isolate the value of spatial correlation specifically? We compare the scheduler fed two covariances that differ in exactly one respect.

The joint covariance \(\hat\Sigma^{\mathrm{joint}}\) is the full empirical covariance. The shuffled covariance \(\hat\Sigma^{\mathrm{shuf}}\) zeros every cross-region block while keeping the within-region blocks intact: \[ \big(\hat\Sigma^{\mathrm{shuf}}\big)_{rs}=\begin{cases}\Sigma_{rr}, & r=s,\\[2pt] \mathbf{0}_{T\times T}, & r\ne s.\end{cases} \]

This operation preserves each region's marginal distribution and its within-region temporal autocorrelation, and destroys only the cross-region dependence. So the only difference between scheduling under \(\hat\Sigma^{\mathrm{joint}}\) and \(\hat\Sigma^{\mathrm{shuf}}\) is whether the optimizer "knows about" spatial correlation. If that knowledge is worth anything, the joint-fitted schedule should achieve a lower out-of-sample tail cost than the shuffled one. The test is deliberately a falsification: it is built to detect a spatial benefit if one exists, so a null is informative rather than a failure to look. block_diagonal_by_region · covariance.py

We report the spatial gap \[ \text{gap}\;=\;\mathrm{CVaR}_{0.95}\big(\text{shuffled}\big)\;-\;\mathrm{CVaR}_{0.95}\big(\text{joint}\big), \] so a positive gap means the joint covariance helped (lower tail emissions). We express it as a percentage of the shuffled CVaR so the two grids (whose absolute emissions differ) are comparable.

5Evaluation protocol

The risk metric — CVaR0.95

A schedule \(x\) fitted on training data is scored on every held-out test day \(i\) by its realized emissions \(e_i=\langle\rho_i,x\rangle\). We care about bad days, so the objective is the Conditional Value-at-Risk at level \(\beta=0.95\) — the mean of the worst \(5\%\) of days: \[ \mathrm{CVaR}_{\beta}(\{e_i\})\;=\;\frac{1}{\lceil (1-\beta)N\rceil}\sum_{i\,:\,e_i\ \text{in worst } (1-\beta)} e_i, \] i.e. sort the \(N\) test-day emissions in descending order and average the top \(\lceil 0.05N\rceil\). This is the operationally relevant quantity: a carbon-aware operator is judged on its worst days, not its average. cvar_upper_tail · experiment scripts

Choosing the radius \(\varepsilon\) — blocked time-series cross-validation

\(\varepsilon\) is selected, separately for the joint and shuffled arms, by 5-fold blocked cross-validation on the training years 2021–2024. The training days are split into 5 contiguous blocks (no shuffling — that would leak the future into the past). For each candidate \(\varepsilon\) on the grid \(\{0,\,0.1,\,1,\,10,\,100,\,1000\}\), we fit \(\hat\Sigma\) and \(\bar\rho\) on four blocks, solve the SOCP, and score the validation CVaR on the held-out block; we pick the \(\varepsilon^\star\) minimizing the mean validation CVaR across folds. The chosen \(\varepsilon^\star\) is then refit on the full training set and the schedule is evaluated once on the untouched 2025 test set.

Uncertainty — paired bootstrap

With ~360 test days, the worst-5 % average has real sampling noise, so we bootstrap a 95 % CI on the gap. We resample test-day indices with replacement (the same indices applied to both arms — a paired comparison), recompute the gap on each of 1000 resamples, and report the 2.5 / 97.5 percentiles. A gap is flagged detectable only if that CI excludes zero.

Pre-registration discipline

For every experiment we (1) ran a dry-run gate that performs the full cross-validation and reports \(\varepsilon^\star\) without touching 2025; (2) commit-locked the configuration in git before any test-set evaluation; (3) read the 2025 test set exactly once, at the very end. This removes the degrees of freedom that let a researcher (even unconsciously) tune toward a desired outcome — the configuration that produced the numbers below is the one that was frozen in advance.

Side-by-side verdict

In both grids the spatial gap is a few hundredths of a percent of CVaR essentially everywhere; neither shows an economically meaningful, robust spatial benefit. Iberia replicates the California null — it does not diverge. The more tightly-coupled MIBEL grid did not surface spatial value.

7Why the result is reliable

It is substantive, not mechanical — the Goldilocks check

A null is only meaningful if the optimizer could have responded to spatial structure but chose not to. If the feasible set were so tight that the schedule were frozen, "no effect" would be a mechanical artifact of the constraints, not a statement about correlation. We verify it is not frozen by measuring how much the schedule reallocates as \(\varepsilon\) grows, on the deterministic optimum: \[ \text{move}(\varepsilon)\;=\;\big\|x(\varepsilon)-x(0)\big\|_1\quad\text{as a fraction of total work.} \]

Across all nine regime × α cells in each grid the optimizer retains genuine room to move — it is appropriately constrained, not frozen:

California (R=4)24–32 %
Iberia + France (R=3)9.5–18 %

Reallocation room (max over ε of the schedule move, as a share of total work). Both well above zero ⇒ the set is not frozen, so the null reflects a genuine absence of exploitable spatial structure. Every constraint is active but not dominant by design (e.g. in R2 the thermal cap binds ~22–26 % of cells, the deadline binds for most regions) — present and realistic, without removing the optimizer's freedom. report_binding_margins_*.py

It survives a robustness battery

Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage. The sample covariance is noisy in the \(p\!\approx\!RT\) vs \(n\) regime; we re-ran with the shrinkage estimator that optimally pulls the noisy off-diagonals toward a scaled identity. Result: the gaps reproduce to ~4 significant figures — shrinkage does not widen the joint-vs-shuffled separation.

Residual-space. Spatial uncertainty may live in forecast errors, not raw levels. We re-estimated the covariance from the residuals of two forecasters — a seasonal (month × hour) baseline and a per-cell AR(1) — keeping the mean field and the scored emissions on raw intensity. Our locked agreement rule counts an effect only if both baselines agree in direction. They do not agree on any beneficial spatial effect in either grid; the only cell where they agree (Iberia R1/α0.75) is the artifact above, where they agree the joint is marginally worse.

It replicates across two independent grids

Two grids with different geography, climate, generation mix and coupling structure — CAISO/NV and MIBEL+France — give the same conclusion. A replicated null is far stronger evidence than a single region: it is unlikely that two independent datasets would both hide a real effect in the same way, given a falsification test designed to expose it.

8Why this makes sense

The key intuition

High marginal correlation does not imply exploitable robust-scheduling value. In Iberia, Spain–Portugal carbon intensity correlates at ≈ 0.85 — higher than any California pair (which sat at 0.43–0.77) — yet the joint covariance buys nothing over the block-diagonal one. Correlation being present in the data and correlation being useful to a worst-case scheduler are different things, and the shuffled-marginals test is precisely what separates them.

Two mechanisms explain it. First, the penalty is a small correction. Cross-validation selects small radii (\(\varepsilon^\star\) typically 0–1), so the robustness term \(\varepsilon\|L^\top\mathrm{vec}(x)\|_2\) is a minor adjustment on top of the mean-cost term \(\langle\bar\rho,x\rangle\), which both arms share identically. The schedule is driven mostly by where carbon is cheap on average; the covariance — joint or shuffled — only nudges it.

Second, the off-diagonal blocks barely move the worst case. What the penalty sees is the quadratic form \(x^\top\hat\Sigma x = \sum_r x_r^\top\Sigma_{rr}x_r + \sum_{r\ne s} x_r^\top\Sigma_{rs}x_s\). For a single fixed schedule concentrated in each region's cheap hours, the diagonal (within-region) terms dominate the sum; zeroing the off-diagonal \(\Sigma_{rs}\) changes the penalty — and therefore the optimal schedule and its tail cost — by very little. Spatial correlation would matter most if the operator could continuously re-allocate between regions in response to a realized joint shock; the present design commits to one representative schedule, which is exactly the setting in which spatial structure has the least leverage (and the most natural place a future positive result could appear — see next).

For a capstone, a carefully-constructed negative result is a real contribution: using a falsification test, on a defensible feasible set, replicated across two grids, we show that the spatial-correlation extension does not pay off for robust CVaR scheduling — and we can say why.

9Limitations & next steps

  • One representative schedule. We fit a single schedule and score it across the test year. A rolling day-ahead formulation that re-optimizes each day against updated forecasts is the most likely place a genuine spatial benefit could emerge — the highest-value next experiment.
  • Single climate parameter re-calibrated. Only the thermal set-point \(T_{\mathrm{set}}\) was changed for Iberia (20 → 14 °C), because the milder annual-mean field would otherwise leave the thermal constraint inactive. The headline regime (R1) carries no thermal assumption at all, so the conclusion does not depend on this choice; we will report the result's invariance to it.
  • Common-clock choice. Portugal (WET) is scheduled on the Spain/France (CET) clock, a one-hour offset. This cannot bias the joint-vs-shuffled comparison (it perturbs both arms identically), but it is documented.
  • Reporting clarity. The "detectable" flag conflates statistical and practical significance; the report tables will add a practical-significance threshold so trivially-tiny but CI-tight cells are not misread.
  • Publication track. Given the replicated null, the Phase-2 vine-copula extension may pivot toward characterizing when spatial structure matters (metric, ambiguity set, or the adaptive setting above) rather than assuming it does.

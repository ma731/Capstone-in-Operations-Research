Does knowing how dirty different power grids get
at the same time help schedule data centers greener?

We spent months rigorously testing this one idea. The answer turned out to be no — and the reason why is the interesting part. This page explains everything we did, in plain language, with the math, the code, and the choices behind it.

1The big idea, in one minute

Data centers use enormous amounts of electricity. But electricity is cleaner at some hours and in some places (when sun and wind are plentiful) and dirtier at others (when power comes from gas or coal). A lot of computer work — training an AI model, processing a batch job — doesn't have to happen right now. It can wait a few hours, or run in a different city.

So a carbon-aware scheduler shifts flexible work toward the cleanest times and places, cutting emissions for free. The animation shows the idea: the gold curve is how dirty the grid is over a day; the blue bars are the computing work, which the scheduler piles into the clean hours.

Gold = carbon intensity over 24 hours. Blue bars = scheduled computing. Watch the work slide toward the cleanest hours.

The question we asked: grids near each other tend to get clean and dirty together (sunshine hits California and Nevada at once). This is called spatial correlation. The hope was that a scheduler which knows this correlation could be smarter — like an investor who diversifies across assets. We built the machinery to test it, and tested it as hard as we could.

The finding: the correlation is absolutely real, but it does not help the schedule. And we figured out exactly why.

2The concepts you need (each in plain words)

Uncertainty — why we can't just "optimize"

We plan tomorrow's schedule, but we don't know exactly how clean tomorrow's power will be. If we bet everything on one forecast and it's wrong, the schedule is bad. So we must plan under uncertainty.

DRO — "plan for the worst plausible case"

Distributionally Robust Optimization (DRO) doesn't bet on a single forecast. It imagines a whole cloud of plausible futures around your best guess and picks the schedule that's safest if reality lands anywhere in that cloud. The size of the cloud is a knob called $\varepsilon$ (epsilon): $\varepsilon=0$ means "trust the forecast exactly," large $\varepsilon$ means "be very paranoid."

Analogy: packing for a trip. Instead of packing for the exact forecast, you pack for the range of weather that's plausible. DRO does that for schedules.

CVaR — "the average of your worst days"

We don't care about a typical day; we care about the bad days. CVaR at 95% means: line up all your days from best to worst, take the worst 5%, and average their emissions. It's a number for "how bad are my bad days." We always try to make this small.

Spatial correlation & the covariance matrix

When two regions get clean and dirty together, they're correlated. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is just a grid of numbers recording how strongly every pair of regions (and hours) moves together. The "cross-region" parts of that grid are the spatial information — the thing we're testing.

Two regions' carbon over time. When they rise and fall together, they're correlated. Toggle the correlation to see the difference.

Copulas — "the shape of moving together"

Correlation is one number, but two things can move together in different shapes. A copula describes the full shape of how variables move together. Key fact for us: a plain covariance can only describe a symmetric, elliptical shape — it assumes things get dirty together exactly as much as they get clean together.

Tail dependence — "clean together vs dirty together"

Real grids are lopsided: they get clean together (sunny everywhere) more than they get dirty together. That lopsidedness is called tail dependence, and a covariance literally cannot represent it. (Remember this — it's half the reason for our result.)

The mean field — "the big daily wave"

The mean field $\bar\rho$ is the predictable average rhythm of carbon: clean around midday (solar), dirty at night. It's a huge effect. Everything else (correlation, randomness) is small ripples on top of this wave. This idea is the heart of our explanation.

3The clever test: "shuffled marginals"

How do you prove whether the spatial correlation helps? You can't just say "the schedule is good." You need a controlled experiment that changes exactly one thing.

So we run the scheduler twice, on covariance matrices that differ in only one way:

  • Joint: the real covariance, with the cross-region correlation intact.
  • Shuffled: the same matrix, but we delete the cross-region parts (set them to zero), keeping every region's own behavior identical.

Then we compare their worst-day emissions (CVaR) on a held-out future year (2025) the scheduler never saw. The difference is the spatial gap. If knowing the correlation helps, "joint" should win and the gap should be positive. If they tie, the correlation is useless.

The covariance matrix. The bright off-diagonal blocks are the cross-region correlation. "Shuffling" zeroes them out — the only change between the two experiments.
Why this design is strong: it's a falsification test — built to detect a benefit if one exists. So when it finds nothing, that "nothing" is informative, not just a failure to look. We also pre-registered it: we locked the settings in code before ever looking at the 2025 test data, so we couldn't fool ourselves into a result (the same discipline used in clinical trials).

4Why the correlation doesn't help (the two reasons)

Reason 1: the mean wave drowns out the ripple

We proved the covariance signal is real — using a trick called mean-ablation. We artificially flattened the daily wave and re-ran. Suddenly the correlation did help (by up to +1.46%)! So the spatial information is genuinely there and exploitable — it's just swamped by the mean wave in the real problem. The scheduler is so busy surfing the giant wave that the ripples don't change its path.

The big gold wave (mean carbon) dominates. The small blue ripples (correlation) ride on top — real, but far too small to steer the schedule.

Reason 2: the covariance is the wrong shape

Even the part of the correlation that does vary is the wrong shape for a covariance. Grids get clean together more than dirty together (lopsided / non-elliptical). A covariance assumes a symmetric shape, so it's blind to this. We measured it: the "dirty-together" coefficient $\chi_U$ is small, the "clean-together" $\chi_L$ is larger. A risk-averse scheduler cares about the dirty tail — where there's nothing to exploit.

In short: the covariance is both masked (drowned by the mean) and mis-shaped (wrong geometry for the real dependence). Two independent reasons, same conclusion.

5"But what if you used a better tool?" — the copula test (Phase 2)

A fair objection: "of course a covariance failed — it's the wrong shape. Use a copula that can capture the lopsided shape." So we did.

We built schedulers using three copulas of increasing power, plus the extreme worst case:

  • Independence — regions decoupled (our baseline).
  • Gaussian — symmetric (what a covariance can see).
  • Clayton — captures "clean together" lopsidedness (the right shape!).
  • Comonotone — the most extreme possible coupling; the absolute ceiling on what any copula could ever achieve.
Each dot is a pair of "how clean was region A vs region B" on a day. Independence = a blob. Gaussian = a symmetric tilt. Clayton = points cluster in the "clean-clean" corner (bottom-left) — the lopsidedness covariance can't see.

Result: still nothing. Even Clayton — the tool built for exactly this shape — and even the extreme comonotone ceiling recover no meaningful value. The biggest "win" we saw turned out to be random noise from how we sampled scenarios (it vanished when we changed the random seed — we caught this and reported it honestly).

What this proves: the null isn't because we used the wrong dependence tool. No passive dependence model — covariance, Gaussian, Clayton, or the maximal coupling — can help. The limit isn't the tool; it's that the mean wave dominates.

6The math, demystified

Don't worry — every symbol has a plain meaning. Here are the only equations that matter.

The scheduler's goal

The DRO scheduler minimizes:

$$\min_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\ \underbrace{\langle\bar\rho,\,x\rangle}_{\text{average emissions}}\;+\;\underbrace{\varepsilon\,\lVert L^\top x\rVert_2}_{\text{safety margin}}$$
  • $x$ = the schedule (how much computing in each region, each hour).
  • $\bar\rho$ = the mean carbon field (the big wave). $\langle\bar\rho,x\rangle$ = average emissions.
  • $\varepsilon$ = how paranoid we are. $L$ comes from the covariance ($LL^\top=\Sigma$); $\lVert L^\top x\rVert_2$ is the "spread" of emissions the schedule is exposed to.
Read it as: "make average emissions low and don't expose yourself to a wide spread of bad outcomes." The first term is the wave; the second is the correlation. Our whole finding is that the first term dominates the second.

Because of a clever rewrite, this is a Second-Order Cone Program (SOCP) — a kind of optimization computers solve to the exact best answer in a fraction of a second.

CVaR — the worst-days measure

$$\mathrm{CVaR}_{\beta}(e)=\min_{\tau}\Big\{\tau+\tfrac{1}{1-\beta}\,\mathbb{E}[(e-\tau)_+]\Big\}$$

You don't need the formula's guts. It just computes "the average of the worst $(1-\beta)$ fraction of days." With $\beta=0.95$, the worst 5%.

The "shuffle" and the gap

$$\Sigma^{\text{shuf}}_{rs}=\begin{cases}\Sigma_{rr}& r=s\\ \mathbf{0}& r\neq s\end{cases}\qquad \text{gap}=\mathrm{CVaR}(\text{shuffled})-\mathrm{CVaR}(\text{joint})$$

The shuffle keeps each region's own block ($r=s$) and zeroes the cross blocks ($r\neq s$). A positive gap = the real correlation helped. We got ≈ 0 everywhere.

Our little theorem (the "mean-dominance bound")

We proved the spatial gap can't be large:

$$\big|\text{gap}\big|\ \le\ \varepsilon\cdot\big(\text{max schedule size}\big)\cdot\sqrt{\lVert\Sigma^{\text{off}}\rVert}$$

In words: the benefit of correlation is bounded by the paranoia knob $\varepsilon$ times the size of the cross-region blocks. It shrinks to zero when there's no robustness ($\varepsilon\to0$) or no cross-correlation. The bound is loose, so we also measured the true ceiling directly (using the comonotone copula): it's under 0.1% — basically zero.

7The code — what each file actually does

The repository is organized so each piece does one clear job. Here's the tour.

The brains (src/models/)

algorithm_2b_mahalanobis.py
The core scheduler. Takes the mean field, the covariance, and the paranoia knob $\varepsilon$, and solves the SOCP for the best schedule. This is the "min … + …" equation above, turned into code.
feasible_set.py
The rules the schedule must obey (capacity limits, ramp limits, deadlines, the flexible/inflexible split). Written once and shared by every scheduler so the comparison is always apples-to-apples.
covariance.py
Builds the covariance matrix from data, does the $LL^\top$ factorization, the Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage, and the all-important block_diagonal_by_region — the function that performs the "shuffle."
cvar_saa.py
The Phase-2 scheduler. Instead of a covariance, it takes a bag of "what-if" future scenarios and minimizes their worst-5% emissions (CVaR) directly. A linear program.
copula_scenarios.py
Generates those "what-if" scenarios under different dependence shapes (independence, Gaussian, Clayton, comonotone) by coupling real historical days — no fake data.
algorithm_1.py
The plain deterministic baseline ($\varepsilon=0$): schedule against the forecast with no robustness. The thing everything else is compared to.

The analysis (src/analysis/)

stratified_correlations.py
Defines the grids (which regions, which time zone) and computes the residual correlations — the "is the assumption even true?" check (it is).
tail_dependence.py
Computes the "clean-together vs dirty-together" coefficients ($\chi_L$, $\chi_U$) — the diagnostic that shows the dependence is the wrong shape for a covariance.
metrics.py
The two tiny shared functions: CVaR of a list of days, and emissions of a schedule on a panel of days. Used everywhere so every number is computed identically.

The experiments (scripts/)

run_case_experiment.py
The main engine. For each grid it does the whole pre-registered protocol: cross-validate $\varepsilon$, fit joint vs shuffled, read the 2025 test set once, bootstrap a confidence interval on the gap, write the results. Flags let you run the robustness battery (--shrinkage, --residualize, --ablate-mean, --ramp-mw, --utilization).
run_copula_experiment.py
The Phase-2 engine: fits the copulas, generates scenarios, solves the CVaR schedules, compares them out-of-sample.
plot_*.py
Every figure in the thesis/poster: correlation heatmaps & map, the headline "finding" chart, the schedule visualization, the robustness forest plot, the cross-validation curves, the copula density & result, the scenario figures.
measure_complexity.py
Times the solver and counts problem sizes for the "is this fast?" paragraph (it is: < 0.3 s per schedule).
bh_correction.py
Applies the Benjamini–Hochberg multiple-testing correction — the statistical guard against finding a fluke just because we ran many tests.

Trust & reproducibility

169 automated tests check every piece (including one that proves the Phase-1 and Phase-2 schedulers use the identical rule set). The licensed carbon data is never committed; only derived summary numbers are saved, so every figure traces to a tracked source.

8Why each choice was the right one (with the papers)

Why Wasserstein DRO?

The "cloud of plausible futures" is built using the Wasserstein distance, which has a beautiful property: the scary-looking "worst case over infinitely many distributions" collapses into the simple, solvable "mean + safety margin" we showed. It also works directly from data without assuming a distribution.

📄 Mohajerin Esfahani & Kuhn (2018), Math. Programming; Blanchet, Kang & Murthy (2019); Kuhn et al. (2019).

Why CVaR (not just average)?

A green scheduler should protect against bad days, not just average ones. CVaR is the standard "coherent" risk measure for exactly this, and it has a clean linear form.

📄 Rockafellar & Uryasev (2000); Artzner, Delbaen, Eber & Heath (1999).

Why the shuffled-marginals test?

It isolates exactly one variable (cross-region correlation) while holding everything else fixed — the gold standard for a clean causal answer. This design is our own methodological contribution.

Why copulas for Phase 2?

Copulas are the mathematically complete way to describe dependence beyond covariance, and Clayton specifically captures the "clean-together" lopsidedness our diagnostic found. Vine copulas make this tractable in high dimensions.

📄 Sklar (1959); Joe (2014); Aas, Czado, Frigessi & Bakken (2009); Dißmann et al. (2013); Czado (2019).

Why these solvers?

We used HiGHS and CLARABEL — free, open-source, reliable, and fast enough that the whole study runs in under two hours on a laptop. (We deliberately did not need a commercial solver, on the supervisor's advice.)

Why extend Hall et al.?

They established single-region Wasserstein DRO for carbon-aware scheduling. Treating carbon as a spatial vector was the natural, previously untested next step.

📄 Hall et al. (2024), arXiv:2410.21510; for the capacity constraints, Wijayawardana & Chien (2025), SoCC'25.

9What we could have done differently (honestly)

Used a full vine copula instead of exchangeable Clayton. Our Clayton forces a single shape for all region pairs, but real pairs differ. A vine copula handles per-pair shapes. Why we didn't: the comonotone (maximal) arm already upper-bounds any copula, vines included — so if the maximum can't help, a vine can't either.
Robustified the dependence structure itself (a "copula-ambiguity" DRO, Fan–Ji–Lejeune 2024) rather than sampling from one fitted copula. Heavier machinery; our bound predicts it would also be dominated by the mean.
Tested more grids and more years. We did walk-forward to 2024 and five region sets; a multi-year rolling evaluation across more continents would strengthen external validity.
Built the active transfer channel. This is the one change that could actually break the null — but it's a whole new project (Part 3) and overlaps a teammate's scope, so it's future work, not a capstone tweak.

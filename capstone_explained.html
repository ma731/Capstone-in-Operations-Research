01In one paragraph

Data centers run a lot of flexible work (think model training and batch jobs) that can wait a few hours or run in a different region. Electricity is "cleaner" at some hours and places than others, because wind, solar, and hydro come and go. A carbon-aware scheduler moves compute toward the clean hours and clean regions to cut emissions.

The field already does this. The interesting question is how fancy the model needs to be. This project builds a real day-ahead scheduler and then prices three optional layers of sophistication, one at a time: (1) letting jobs migrate between regions, (2) modelling how regions' carbon levels move together, and (3) hedging against forecast error with distributionally robust optimization. The result is a clean separation: one layer pays, two mostly do not.

02The big question

Every extra layer of modelling costs something: more data to estimate, more compute, more things that can go wrong. It is only worth it if it buys measurably better schedules. The literature mostly assumed that richer models help. This thesis measures it directly, and asks a value-first question: once you have a working scheduler, which extra layer is worth its price?

The yardstick throughout is CVaR₀.₀₅: the average emissions on the worst 5% of days. We care about the bad days, not the average day, because that is what a risk-averse operator wants to protect against.

03The three answers

RQ1 · the lever that pays

Letting compute move between regions

Active inter-region migration cuts worst-day emissions by 4.0% to 9.9% over a like-for-like no-transfer baseline (Western 4.0%, Eastern 9.9%, Diversified 9.0%). The driver is simple: at each hour, one region is cleanest on average, so you ship work there. This is the headline saving, and it does not need any fancy dependence model.

Honest footnote: the migration mechanism itself is established practice in carbon-aware computing. The contribution is the careful, like-for-like measurement of what it is worth.

RQ2 · the layer to skip

Modelling how regions co-move (covariance, copulas)

Across grids spanning the full range from near-independent to strongly correlated, modelling the joint structure adds essentially zero (below 0.4% of CVaR), and it survives a full battery of robustness checks. The co-movement is real, but it is dwarfed by the differences in average carbon, and the part that varies is the wrong shape for this kind of model to use.

RQ3 · the conditional option

Hedging forecast error with robust optimization

Distributional robustness only starts to pay once a rare emergency makes a region's carbon spike by about 3× (the crossover M⋆≈3). Real worst-case emergencies across 17 zones only reach about 1.4×, so on observed conditions the simpler deterministic scheduler wins. Even at high severity the crossover survives careful multi-seed testing on only one grid, and only marginally.

The practitioner rule: deploy a per-region scheduler plus an active inter-region transfer channel (that captures the value); treat a richer dependence model and a robust layer as conditional options, not free wins.

04How it was tested

Two clean experiments carry the argument. Both keep the optimizer fixed and change exactly one thing, so any difference is attributable to that one thing.

The shuffled-marginals test (does dependence add value?)

Fit the scheduler twice: once with the real cross-region links intact, and once with those links destroyed but each region's own pattern left perfectly intact. Compare the worst-day emissions. The difference is the value of the cross-region structure. It came out at essentially zero, and the whole thing was pre-committed (the analysis plan was locked in version control before the test data was ever read), so the result cannot be massaged after the fact.

The mean-leveling test (is the null real, or is the test just blind?)

This is the clever safeguard. We deliberately flatten each region's average carbon level, so the only signal left is the cross-region co-movement, and re-run. In this constructed world the dependence layer does pay (up to +1.46%). That is the test's positive control: it proves the test can detect value when value exists. So the zero we measure on real data is a genuine "no", not a blunt instrument that cannot see anything.

05The honest headline

This project is unusual in that its central result is a careful null: a rigorous "this fancier thing does not actually help here", reported honestly rather than hidden. That is harder to attack than an inflated positive, and it is paired with a real, measured saving (the transfer lever) and a clear decision rule.

06A note on the word "spatial"

"Spatial" here means across the operator's geographically separate regions (sites), exactly as in the standard spatial-versus-temporal load-shifting distinction (move work to a cleaner place vs. a cleaner hour).

It does not mean distance-based spatial autocorrelation (no Moran's I, no variograms, no distance-decay), and it is not an electrical coupling of the grids, which need not be connected at all. The dependence we study is just the ordinary statistical cross-region correlation of carbon intensity, driven by shared weather and shared generation. The thesis says "cross-region correlation" for the statistic and reserves "spatial" for the across-locations decision.

07The real contribution

The most novel and most defensible part is not any one equation (those are standard tools). It is a reusable recipe for pricing a modelling layer, plus the discipline that makes its answer trustworthy:

  1. Separate validity from value. The cross-region correlations are genuinely real (0.7 to 0.8). That does not mean modelling them helps the decision. The thesis measures the two separately, and they come apart.
  2. The value-pricing protocol. Fix the optimizer, perturb exactly one modelling object, pre-commit, and read off its marginal worst-day value. Only the perturbed object changes when you apply it to a different problem, so the recipe transfers to any correlated-uncertainty optimization, not just carbon.
  3. A cheap pre-screen. A simple ratio (how big the average-carbon swings are versus the co-movement) flags, before touching any data, when a dependence layer cannot possibly matter.

Together these turn "is this layer worth modelling?" from a matter of faith into a checkable, pre-committed measurement.

08Why it matters (what it enables)

For a practitioner, the four-layer decision rule says where to spend effort and where not to:

LayerWhat it isVerdict
1Per-region carbon-aware scheduling (shift work to clean hours)Deploy always
2Active inter-region transfer (ship work to clean regions)Deploy if you can migrate (this is the 4–9.9%)
3Modelling the joint covariance / copulaSkip (adds ≈0)
4Robustifying against forecast errorOnly if you expect severe emergencies

In money terms, for an Eastern US–Canada facility the transfer lever is worth on the order of $1.7M/yr in avoided carbon, while the covariance-modelling channel is about $37,000/yr and not even reliably positive. The value lives in the transfer decision and the mean forecast, not the second moment.

09Plain-English glossary

Every technical term in the thesis, in human words, with an everyday analogy where it helps.

Carbon intensity: how dirty the electricity is right now, in grams of CO₂ per kilowatt-hour. It swings hour by hour and region by region with how much wind, solar, and hydro are on the grid.
Carbon-aware scheduling: deciding when and where to run flexible jobs so they land in the cleanest hours and regions.
Day-ahead: committing tomorrow's plan today, using a forecast, because in practice you cannot wait for each hour to arrive.
CVaR₀.₀₅ how bad the bad days are: Conditional Value-at-Risk: the average emissions across the worst 5% of days, not the average over all days.
DRO robust planning: distributionally robust optimization: instead of trusting one forecast, optimize against the worst plausible forecast in a whole cloud of them. Like packing for weather a little worse than predicted.
Wasserstein ball: the "cloud of plausible futures": all the probability distributions within a chosen distance of the data. The radius (epsilon) is the single dial for how cautious the plan is.
Mahalanobis distance: a distance that accounts for how regions move together, so two correlated regions count as "closer". Like measuring distance on a stretched map.
SOCP: second-order cone program: a well-behaved convex problem that solvers crack fast and to a guaranteed best answer. The robust scheduler reduces to one.
Copula: the part of a joint distribution that captures only how variables move together, with each one's own behaviour stripped out.
Comonotone copula: the most extreme coupling possible: every region rising and falling in perfect lockstep. Used as a worst-case ceiling: if even perfect synchrony adds nothing, nothing weaker will.
Tail dependence (χL, χU): whether regions hit their extremes at the same time. Here regions sync up when clean but decouple when dirty, an effect plain correlation cannot see, and the wrong way round for a risk-averse scheduler.
Cross-region correlation: the ordinary statistical correlation between regions' carbon-intensity series (see the note on "spatial" above). Real here, at 0.7 to 0.8.
Residualization: removing each region's predictable daily pattern so the analysis works only on the genuine surprises, not the routine day-night cycle.
Shuffled-marginals test: fit the scheduler twice, once with the real cross-region links and once with them destroyed but each region's own pattern intact, then compare worst-day emissions. The difference is the value of the cross-region structure.
Mean-leveling test the positive control: deliberately flatten each region's average carbon and re-run, so the only signal left is the co-movement. It shows the structure can pay in a constructed world, which proves the real-data "zero" is a true negative, not a blind test.
Mean-dominance: the central reason for the null: the swing in average carbon is so much larger than the cross-region co-movement that any covariance or copula model can only move the schedule by a provably tiny amount.
Out-of-sample / walk-forward: scoring a plan only on data it never trained on; walk-forward is the rolling version (train on earlier years, test on a later untouched one), mimicking real deployment.
Equivalence test (TOST): a test that affirmatively shows an effect is smaller than a chosen materiality margin, turning "we found nothing" into "any effect this large is ruled out".
Severity M and the crossover M⋆: M is how many times worse carbon spikes in a rare emergency (M = 3 is a tripling). M⋆ ≈ 3 is where the robust layer starts to beat the simple plan; real emergencies only reach about M = 1.4.
Value of the stochastic solution (VSS): how much you gain by planning against the full spread of uncertainty rather than a single best-guess forecast. Here it is near zero.
Transfer budget (Φ): the dial for how much compute may migrate between regions. Φ = 0 is the honest no-transfer baseline; turning it up delivers the 4–9.9% saving.

10Defense quick-answers

Short, clear answers to the questions an examiner is most likely to ask.

"What is M, and what is the 1.4?"
M is the multiple by which a region's carbon intensity spikes in a rare grid emergency: M = 3 means three times dirtier than normal. The robust layer only earns its keep above about M = 3. When we measure the worst real emergencies across 17 zones, the joint severity only reaches about 1.4×, well short of where robustness would pay.
"What is the mean-leveling test?"
We artificially flatten each region's average carbon so the only thing left is how the regions move together, then re-run the scheduler. It is a controlled check: if the cross-region structure ever pays, it pays here, and it does (+1.46%). That is why we can say the real-data null is a true "no value", not a test too blunt to see it.
"Isn't a null result just a failure?"
No. It is a specification finding: a precise, pre-committed statement that a popular extra layer does not pay here, backed by a positive control and a money-valued decision rule. It saves practitioners from estimating models that cannot help.
"Is 'spatial correlation' the right term?"
We use "cross-region correlation" for the statistic, and reserve "spatial" for the across-locations decision (spatial vs temporal shifting). It is deliberately not distance-based spatial autocorrelation (Moran's I) and not an electrical coupling of the grids.
"What is genuinely new?"
A reusable, pre-committed protocol for pricing any single modelling layer in a stochastic program, separating whether an assumption is true from whether it is useful, applied to carbon scheduling and certified with a cheap a-priori pre-screen and a positive control.
